1.2 What Is Pharmacokinetics?

Pharmacokinetics (PK) answers the question: What does the body do to the drug?

It describes the time course of drug concentration in the body — how the drug is absorbed, distributed, metabolized, and excreted (ADME). Pharmacokinetics quantifies:

  • Absorption: How the drug enters systemic circulation from the site of administration
  • Distribution: How the drug spreads throughout tissues and organs
  • Metabolism: How enzymes (primarily in the liver) chemically modify the drug
  • Excretion: How the drug and its metabolites are eliminated (kidneys, bile, lungs)
Code 
# A simple conceptual PK simulation
# Visualizing concentration over time after an IV bolus
library(ggplot2)

time <- seq(0, 24, by = 0.1)
dose <- 100  # mg
Vd   <- 10   # L (volume of distribution)
ke   <- 0.3  # 1/h (elimination rate constant)

conc <- (dose / Vd) * exp(-ke * time)

ggplot(data.frame(time, conc), aes(time, conc)) +
  geom_line(color = "#0B6E6B", linewidth = 1.2) +
  labs(title = "Simulated Drug Concentration After IV Bolus",
       subtitle = "One-compartment model with first-order elimination",
       x = "Time (hours)", y = "Concentration (mg/L)") +
  theme_minimal()

1.3 What Is Pharmacodynamics?

Pharmacodynamics (PD) answers the question: What does the drug do to the body?

It describes the relationship between drug concentration at the site of action and the resulting pharmacological effect — both therapeutic and toxic. Pharmacodynamics involves:

  • Receptor binding: Drug molecules interacting with cellular targets
  • Signal transduction: How receptor activation leads to cellular response
  • Dose-response: The relationship between dose (or concentration) and effect magnitude
  • Potency and efficacy: How much drug is needed and what maximum effect it can produce

1.4 What Is Pharmacometrics?

Pharmacometrics is the science of quantitative pharmacology — the integration of PK, PD, and disease progression using mathematical and statistical models. It is the engine behind:

  • Model-informed drug development (MIDD)
  • Optimal dose selection
  • Clinical trial simulation
  • Regulatory decision support

Pharmacometrics uses R extensively, making it a core skill for modern pharmaceutical scientists.

1.4.1 The ADME Framework

Process Definition Key Parameter
Absorption Drug enters bloodstream k_a, F (bioavailability)
Distribution Drug spreads through body V_d (volume of distribution)
Metabolism Drug chemically modified CL_{int} (intrinsic clearance)
Excretion Drug removed from body CL (clearance), k_e

1.5 PK vs. PD Questions: Real Examples

1.5.1 Example Scenarios

Question Type Why It Matters
How long does the drug stay in the body? PK Determines dosing frequency
What is the minimum effective concentration? PD Sets the target for dosing
Does food affect absorption? PK Guides administration instructions
Is the drug more potent than the standard? PD Informs dose selection in trials
Does kidney function alter clearance? PK Guides dose adjustment in renal impairment
What is the relationship between dose and side effects? PD Determines therapeutic window

1.6 Why Use R for PK/PD?

R has become a leading platform for PK/PD analysis because:

  1. Comprehensive PK packages: PK, PKNCA, nlmixr2, mrgsolve, rxode2 — purpose-built for pharmacometrics
  2. Reproducibility: Scripts, Quarto, and R Markdown ensure every analysis is traceable
  3. Statistical power: Mixed-effects models (nlme, lme4, saemix) for population PK
  4. Visualization: ggplot2 for publication-quality PK/PD plots
  5. Open source: No licensing costs, community-driven development
  6. Regulatory acceptance: FDA and EMA accept R-based analyses

  2. For each of the following, classify as PK or PD:
    • A drug has a half-life of 12 hours
    • 50% of patients respond at a dose of 10 mg
    • The drug is 95% protein-bound
    • The maximum effect plateaus at 80% inhibition
  3. List three advantages of using R for PK/PD analysis compared to commercial software.
  4. Explain the difference between potency and efficacy.

1.7.2 Common Mistakes

  • Confusing PK with PD: PK = concentration over time (body → drug); PD = effect vs concentration (drug → body)
  • Thinking models are reality: All models are simplifications. A model is useful if it answers the question at hand
  • Ignoring units: Always check that your units are consistent. Mixing mg, μg, and ng is a frequent source of error
  • Skipping exploratory plots: Always plot your concentration-time and dose-response data before modeling

1.7.3 Key Takeaway

Pharmacokinetics describes drug movement (ADME); pharmacodynamics describes drug effect. Together they form the basis for model-informed drug development. R provides a powerful, reproducible environment for PK/PD analysis at every level — from exploratory plots to regulatory submissions.
